The birth of a child is a wonderful but, at the same time, very complex process. It is the cause of many physical and emotional changes both in mother and baby.

The newborn must make many important physical adaptions in a very short time in order to live outside the body of its mother. Leaving the uterus, it can no longer rely on the placenta and on the bloodstream of the mother for its basic functions. Before birth, breathing, eating, resolving of the products of fetal metabolism, immune protection, all were coming from the mother. When a baby comes in the world, many body systems must change their way of operation dramatically in comparison with the way they were operating during fetal life.     

            

The lungs must breathe air, the heart circulation must change, the digestive system must start to process food and discard the remains, the kidneys must start working to balance fluids and chemicals in the body, liver and the immune system must start functioning independently. All the systems of the body of your baby should cooperate in a new way.    

Sometimes a baby may find it difficult to make this transition to the outside world. Being born prematurely, having suffered a difficult birth process, being born with a congenital abnormality, all these may be factors that make this transition even more difficult or problematic.  This is why 10 to 15% of all newborn babies require NICU care. Fortunately for these newborns, the special and personalized attention that they need is now available in Neonatal Intensive Care Unit (NICU).    

Let’s examine in more detail, what type of babies need this special care:  The most babies entering NICU are premature babies (born before 37 weeks of gestation), they have low birth weight (less than 2.2 kg) or they have another medical problem that requires special treatment. In the U.S., nearly 13% of babies are born prematurely and many of these babies also have low birth weight. Twins, triplets and other babies coming from multiple pregnancies are also often entering the NICU, as they tend to be born earlier and with less weight than other babies. Babies with medical conditions such  as heart problems , infections, or congenital abnormalities also need NICU care.    
Each newborn’s situation is evaluated individually to determine the need of hospitalization in NICU. Nevertheless there are factors that may increase the chances of a baby to be hospitalized in NICU.

Below are listed such high risk factors:

Factors derived from the mother:

  1. • Age under 16 or over 40 years

  2. • Drug or alcohol use

  3. • Diabetes

  4. • Hypertension (high blood pressure)

  5. • Bleeding

  6. • Sexually transmitted diseases

  7. • Multiple pregnancy (twins, triplets or more)

  8. • Too little or too much amniotic fluid.

  9. • Premature rupture of amniotic sac

   
Factors associated with childbirth:

  1. • Fetal distress / perinatal asphyxia (changes in organic systems of the baby due to the lack of oxygen of the newborn)

  2. • Breech at birth (buttocks exit first) or the unusual position output during childbirth

  3. • Meconium (the babies first stools passed during pregnancy into the amniotic fluid)

  4. • Lap of the umbilical cord (the umbilical cord wrapped around baby’s neck)

  5. • Tongs, forceps or cesarean section during labor.


Factors associated with the baby:

  1. • Birth at gestational age less then 37 weeks or greater than 42 weeks.

  2. • Birth weight less than 2.500 grams or more than 4.000 grams

  3. • Small size for gestational age

  4. • Medication or resuscitation in the delivery room

  5. • Genetic abnormalities

  6. • Respiratory distress including rapid breathing, snorting and apnea (stopping breathing)

  7. • Infections such as herpes, group B streptococcus and chlamydia.

  8. •  Seizures

  9. • Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar)

  10. • Need for extra oxygen or extra monitoring

  11. • Need for intravenous (IV) therapy, or other specific medicines

  12. •  Need for special treatment or special medical procedures, such as blood transfusions.

The babies who need to be hospitalized in the NICU, usually go there immediately after their birth or within the first 24 hours from birth.

One case in which a newborn can be hospitalized in the NICU during the second or third day of his life or even later, is  to suffer from intense jaundice. Jaundice is the characteristic yellowing of the skin that occurs as a result of increased levels of bilirubin in the blood.

Jaundice is very common in the newborns and it occurs due to the fact that the liver of newborns is not mature enough to handle the bilirubin. It is common that bilirubin displays peaks between the second and fourth day of a newborn’s life, but if it is too high or it does not go away easily, it can be a sign of a more serious problem. When bilirubin levels are very high,
treatment consists of placing the newborn under a strong blue light bulb, that accelerates the degradation of bilirubin.
