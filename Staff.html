One of the first questions of the parents who learn that their child is in NICU is: “And who is taking care of our baby in the intensive care?”

From its nature, NICU requires continuous presence and cooperation of many dedicated scientists and well-trained support staff of many disciplines in order to deal successfully with the problems of the newborns that can be complex.

The most common specialities that you will meet in the NICU are:

Neonatologist:

A pediatrician can solve most common health problems of newborns, but neonatologists are specially trained to handle most complex problems and high risk situations.

If your newborn child is premature, or has a serious illness, injury or congenital abnormality, the presence of a neonatologist can help both at the time of birth and also during the subsequent care of the newborn.
  

The neonatologists generally provide the following services:

  1. • Diagnosis and treatment of newborns with problems such as breathing disorders, infections, and genetic abnormalities.

  2. • Stabilization and treatment of newborns with any life-threatening medical problems.

  3. • To coordinate the care and medical management of infants born preterm, critically ill or needing surgery.

  4. • To make sure that despite the critical situation that may be in, newborns receive the proper nutrition to heal their problems and ensure their development.

  5. • They also offer services on the delivery rooms in caesarean sections or other types of birth when there are medical problems in the mother or the baby that could affect the baby’s health and put it at risk and so medical intervention is required in the delivery room. 

NICU nurse:

Nurses on the NICU bear the main daily care of a baby in intensive care and often become good angels for parents who have many questions and are very concerned about the status of their baby.

Here are just some of the tasks contained in the daily list of a nurse at NICU. Babies, even the tiniest of them, need regular changes, feeding and cuddling. Usually,  the nurse will go through every baby at regular intervals throughout the day and night time (usually every 3 or 4 hours) and every time she will change the baby’s diaper, she will take baby’s temperature and she will feed it. If a newborn is taking medicines, they can also be administered by the nurse.

     
 

Each nurse in NICU is specialized and is constantly trained to be ready for any drug use in neonates, for the insertion and the change of intravenous lines, for being able to check the proper functioning of ventilators, incubators and the rest of the complex technical equipment of NICU. She is also able to administer shots and blood transfusions when needed and generally she has all the knowledge and skills necessary for the immediate care of the tiny patients.

    The nurses of NICU often have an important role in supporting the parents of the baby at a time when they are very worried and anxious. When possible, parents and sometimes
other family members are invited to take an active role in child care.  Nurses can teach parents how to take care of their infants  properly and according to the babies’ needs and they are watching them as they learn. Usually are those who feel the greatest satisfaction when seeing some of the smallest or sickest babies to stabilize, grow and eventually go home with their family.

In NICU you can also find a range of other specialists for infants (pediatric cardiologist, ophthalmologist, ENT, pediatric neurologist, etc.) which act as consultants. Their help might also  be asked by the neonatologist in order to treat more effectively the problems of the newborns.
